Watch the two dials spin in lockstep. The picture exists because both discs agree — moment by moment — on exactly which pixel is being transmitted. There is no frame buffer, no stored image. The picture is a real-time agreement between two spinning wheels.

Slip the receiver's disc even a few degrees behind the camera's and every row shifts sideways; slip it further and the image begins to roll. Early systems sent a brief synchronising pulse baked into the signal itself — a marker that told the receiver's motor when each new frame began.

Baird's earliest systems used the 50 Hz mains frequency as a synchronisation reference, locking both motors to the electricity supply. It was brittle: if either motor loaded differently under heat, the picture would drift and roll. Use the offset slider to see it happen.

The selenium photocell had a problem no amount of clever design could fully remove. Selenium's resistance does not change instantaneously in response to light — it adjusts with a small chemical delay, somewhere between five and fifty milliseconds depending on temperature and history.

That delay smears every sharp brightness transition into a gradual ramp. A hard edge between a bright wall and a dark shadow — a single pixel's worth of contrast — arrives at the amplifier as a soft slope, trailing the true brightness by a few milliseconds.

Baird's engineers countered this with a passive two-branch network. Alongside the main resistor path carrying the cell's lagging current, they wired a capacitor. A capacitor passes only changing signals — it produces a voltage proportional to the rate of change of the input, the derivative — and that derivative is added back onto the slow selenium response.

The derivative of a gradual ramp is a spike — a brief, sharp pulse at the moment of transition, exactly where the original edge was. Summing the spike with the lagging ramp restores the sharp step. Drag the capacitor-branch slider up and watch the leading edges snap back into definition on the total output.

Too much correction and the network overshoots. The bright side of each edge gets a brief flare that was never in the original picture. The optimum sits where edges are crisp but the overshoot stays just below the threshold of visibility — around 0.85 on the slider here.

The principle underlying this circuit is still in use. FM radio broadcasting pre-emphasises the high-frequency content of the audio signal before transmission, then uses a complementary de-emphasis filter at the receiver. The mathematics are identical; only the application has changed.

Set aside the image and watch what the system actually does. At any given instant there is only one illuminated point in the window — one hole aligned with the aperture, one lamp at full brightness, one pixel being scanned and reconstructed simultaneously.

At low disc speed you can follow that single dot moving through the window: bright where the scene is bright, dim where it is dark, tracing the face from left to right, top to bottom, one slow step at a time. The picture does not exist as a picture. It exists only as a sequence of moments.

Spin the disc faster. At around ten frames per second, the dot completes its journey faster than the eye can register each individual step. The afterimage of each flash lingers just long enough for the next one to overlap it. Your visual system, tricked by persistence of vision, fuses the sequence into a stable image.

Baird's 1926 demonstrations ran thirty lines at twelve and a half frames per second. Thirty lines is roughly what you can resolve in a face-sized picture from a metre away. The image was grainy, dim, and confined to a window smaller than a playing card — but it was unmistakably a face, and it moved.

The fundamental ceiling of mechanical television was light. More lines meant more holes and a faster motor, but smaller holes meant less light through each aperture, which meant a weaker signal, which meant more amplification, which meant more noise. The available lamp power, the photocell sensitivity, and the motor speed formed a triangle of constraints the spinning disc could never escape.

Mechanical television brought a fresh engineering problem that had nothing to do with optics or mechanics. The amplifiers of the 1920s were AC-coupled: they could faithfully carry a vibrating signal but had no ability to hold a steady voltage. Leave a signal at a constant level for more than a few milliseconds and it would droop toward zero.

This is not a flaw in the amplifier design — it is inherent to transformer coupling or capacitor coupling between stages, which was the standard approach because it isolated each stage's DC bias from the next. The amplifier was designed to be AC-coupled. The problem was that television needed DC.

A flat white patch in the picture produces exactly the steady voltage level that an AC amplifier refuses to carry. Detail — rapid variation between adjacent bright and dark pixels — came through fine, because variation is AC. But uniform brightness, the level that distinguishes white from grey from black across a large area, drowned in the droop.

The solution was a chopper: a slotted wheel mounted on the same shaft as the Nipkow disc, placed in the light beam before the photocell. The wheel interrupts the beam at a fixed rate — a few dozen to a few hundred times per second — converting every brightness level, including a steady white, into a rapid pulse train.

An AC amplifier happily carries a pulse train. The information about absolute brightness is encoded in the average height of the pulses. A detector at the far end reads that height back out and lights the lamp accordingly. Nothing is lost, and the amplifier's DC blindness stops mattering.

Toggle the chopper on and watch the uniform white patch. Without it the amplifier output droops to the baseline and the reconstructed strip loses all brightness information — white, grey, and black all flatten to the same ambiguous level. With the chopper running, the white patch becomes a steady tall pulse train and the receiver gets the right answer.

The chopper speed controls the carrier frequency. Slower chopping puts the carrier at lower frequency where amplifiers work cleanly, but gives fewer samples per pixel, limiting how fast brightness can change within a line. Faster chopping risks the amplifier starting to roll off the pulse tops. The slider trades one against the other.

By the mid-1930s, fully electronic camera tubes — the iconoscope, the emitron — were capturing far more detail, storing a whole frame's worth of charge on a photosensitive plate and reading it out electronically. No moving parts. No chopper. No motor to keep in step. And far more lines.

Broadcasters in Britain ran both mechanical and electronic systems in parallel through 1936 so audiences could compare directly. By February 1937 the BBC had shut down the mechanical transmissions entirely, settling on the 405-line fully electronic standard. The spinning disc had lasted eleven years as a broadcast technology.

Every principle the Nipkow disc introduced passed directly into electronic television and then into digital imaging. Line-by-line raster scanning, brightness-to-voltage conversion, sync pulses, AC pre-emphasis, persistence-of-vision frame rates — each one survives in the systems we use today, decades after the last broadcast disc stopped spinning.
